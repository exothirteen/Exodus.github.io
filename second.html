The Triple Spiral popped up in many cultures across the globe between the Neolithic to Bronze Age. One of the earliest occurrences took place between 4400–3600 BC on the island of Malta. It was also found carved into the tomb of Newgrange in Ireland, which was constructed around 3200 BC. Interestingly, it was also found on Greek ships from the Mycenaean phase of the Bronze Age. The Triple Spiral made many appearances across Europe over the years. One of the more notable examples is in a carving in a settlement in the northwestern regions of the Iberian Peninsula. There’s also evidence of the symbol in Gothic architecture which was popular during the 12th to 16th century. As is the case with many Celtic Knots and symbols, the Triskele meaning varies greatly, depending on what you ready and who you speak to. Records from back when these symbols were used are light to say the least, so interpretation plays a big part in deciphering the Celtic Triskele symbol meaning. [1]

Meaning
1.) The Celts believed that everything that was truly important came in threes.
2.) The three spirals represent the three worlds: the present physical realm, the spirit world of ancestors, and the celestial world of the sun, moon, stars, and planets.
3.) The flowing knot design represents the Celt’s all-important number three along with endless lines with no visible point of start or finish.
4.) Some believe the Celtic Triskelion meaning revolves around strength and progress and the ability to move forward and overcome extreme adversity (represented by the appearance of movement in the symbol).
